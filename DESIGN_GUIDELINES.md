### **Website Design Checklist for Claude Code**

#### **1. Messaging and Value Proposition**
*   **DO clearly define the "Who, What, and Why" immediately:** The hero section (the top part of the page) must explain in seconds what the site is, that it is for K12 students, and why they should use it.
*   **DO use specific, descriptive language:** Use terms like "Math Mini-Games" or "Science Quests" instead of vague marketing jargon like "integrated learning platform" or "educational ecosystem".
*   **DO treat the homepage as a "Front Page":** Provide just enough information to intrigue the student without overwhelming them with every available feature or game.
*   **DONT use confusing jargon:** Avoid technical or "ambitious" language intended for investors; keep the copy focused on what the student or teacher actually does on the site.
*   **DONT provide too many equal-weight choices:** Avoid having multiple primary buttons (Call to Actions) in the same view. Focus the user's attention on one main goal, like "Start Playing".

#### **2. Visual Design and Hierarchy**
*   **DO establish a tight information hierarchy:** Ensure the most important message (e.g., the name of a game) is the largest and most prominent element.
*   **DO use high-resolution, high-quality assets:** Blurry or low-quality graphics instantly signal a lack of credibility and care.
*   **DO create an "airy and breathable" layout:** Avoid unnecessary hard borders, dividers, or cluttered boxes. Allow content to feel like it is floating naturally on the page.
*   **DONT default to "AI Slop" trends:** Avoid overused design patterns such as generic **purple gradients**, massive drop-shadows, or the standard "Google-colored" (red/yellow/green/blue) icon palettes.
*   **DONT use low-contrast text:** Never use light gray text on a white background. Ensure high contrast so that the site is easily readable for students of all ages.

#### **3. Interaction and Animation**
*   **DO use motion purposefully:** Use animation to guide the student’s eye toward the most important element, such as a new game unlock or a "Join" button.
*   **DO use standard hover effects:** Interactive elements should feel "clickable" through slight glows or color shifts, rather than disappearing or fading out when a user hovers over them.
*   **DONT "hijack" the scroll:** Never override the browser's native scrolling behavior (scroll-jacking). It makes the website feel clunky and slow, which is frustrating for users.
*   **DONT use distracting background animations:** Avoid looping movements (like floating objects or "meteors") that draw attention away from the text the student is trying to read.
*   **DONT hide critical functions behind hovers:** Important navigation or tools should be visible by default, as hover states do not work on mobile or tablet devices.

### **Final Recommendations for K12 Student Engagement**

1.  **Prioritise a Storytelling Approach:** Instead of selling a "product," tell a story of transformation. Use visuals that show a student starting a journey and the "after" effect of mastering a concept through play.
2.  **Use Human Faces and Relatability:** Students connect with other students. Use high-quality photos or relatable character illustrations to make the brand feel authentic and approachable.
3.  **Optimize for Snappy Performance:** K12 students often use lower-powered devices or school networks. Avoid heavy, unoptimized images that slow down the "First Impression" or make the site feel like "molasses".
4.  **Incorporate "Playful Mystery":** Instead of showing a dry screenshot of a game menu, show a mysterious or exciting preview of a game world to build curiosity and entice the student to explore further.
5.  **Respect Platform Norms:** Do not reinvent how menus or buttons work. Use familiar layouts so students can navigate the site intuitively without needing instructions.
